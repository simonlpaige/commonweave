## The Thing Nobody Is Saying Out Loud

The machines are taking the work. Not eventually -- now, and accelerating. This is not a prediction. It's what's happening. AI writes code, drafts legal briefs, reads medical images, composes music, and generates marketing copy at a quality that's good enough and a cost approaching zero. Robotic systems sort packages, build cars, harvest crops. The automation curve bends fast.

The interesting question is not *whether* this happens. It's *who gets the benefit*.

Right now the answer is: whoever owns the machines. A logistics company automates its warehouse, fires 2,000 workers, and the profit flows to shareholders. A hospital system deploys AI diagnostic tools, cuts radiology staff, and the savings become executive bonuses. This is not a conspiracy. It's just how the current system distributes gains from productivity -- toward capital, not labor.

That's a design choice. You could design it differently.

This project is an attempt to think carefully about what "differently" actually means. Not as a slogan. As a real engineering problem: what systems would need to exist, how do you build them, what's already working at small scale, and how do you get from here to there without everything going badly wrong in the middle.

It's not a manifesto. Manifestos are about arguing. This is about building.

## What We're Trying to Build Toward

Not utopia. Utopia requires angels. We are working with humans.

The target is something more specific: a world where the basic conditions of a decent life -- food, shelter, healthcare, education, a voice in the decisions that affect you -- are not contingent on whether your labor is economically valuable to someone else. Right now those things are contingent on that. As automation expands, that contingency becomes a trap for more and more people.

We also live on a planet with physical limits. Any framework that solves the distribution problem while ignoring the ecological problem is just rearranging deck chairs. So the target includes economic activity that stays within what the earth can actually sustain.

Neither of these targets requires perfection. They require better systems than the ones we have.

## The Honest Framing: Selective Abundance, Not Post-Scarcity

This framework does not claim we're headed for a world where everything is unlimited and free. Some things genuinely are becoming cheap enough to share widely: information, renewable energy, basic nutrition, digital services. Other things remain genuinely scarce and will stay that way: land, fresh water, skilled human care, rare materials, and -- this one is underrated -- attention.

The honest framing is *selective abundance*: some things are becoming abundant and the challenge is distributing them. Others remain scarce and the challenge is governing them fairly. Still others (meaning, connection, purpose) are the scarcities that automation creates even as it eliminates material ones, and those require cultural responses, not just economic ones.

This matters because it shapes what "the transition" actually looks like. It's not a single switch that flips. It's a slow migration across many fronts, at different speeds, in different places.

## Core Principles

These are the load-bearing walls. If you disagree with them, you're working on a different project -- which is fine, but that's what it is.

**1. Universal Sufficiency**
Every person has an unconditional right to food, shelter, healthcare, education, and meaningful participation in society. Not as a privilege. Not contingent on employment. Unconditional.

**2. Ecological Equilibrium**
No economic activity may degrade the systems that sustain life. The economy operates within planetary boundaries -- not because we're anti-growth, but because growth that eats the floor you're standing on is not growth.

**3. Democratic Sovereignty**
Power flows from people, not capital. Decisions are made by those affected by them. This is the classical promise of democracy. The framework takes it seriously enough to mean it.

**4. Common Ownership of the Commons**
Land, water, air, energy, data, and infrastructure belong to everyone. They cannot be privately hoarded. The commons are not "government property." They are shared stewardship.

**5. Voluntary Contribution**
Work is not coerced. People contribute because they find meaning, not because survival depends on it. This does not mean no one does unpleasant work -- it means no one does it at gunpoint.

**6. Non-Violence**
The transition happens through preparation, legitimacy, and collective action -- not force. Non-violence does not mean passive. It means the kind of force we use is nonviolent.

**7. Transparency by Default**
Systems of governance and resource allocation operate in the open. "By default" is doing real work here: exceptions matter. Individual medical records stay private. Whistleblowers and dissidents get protection. Survivors of domestic violence don't get their location published. Children's data is not public. Contributors under authoritarian regimes use whatever security they need.

Transparency applies to *power and resources*, not to *people made vulnerable by visibility*. The failure mode of "radical transparency" is surveillance dressed as accountability -- and the framework explicitly rejects that trade.

## These Principles Are in Tension

Pretending otherwise would be dishonest.

What happens when a democratic majority votes to allow resource extraction that violates ecological limits? What happens when "voluntary contribution" means nobody volunteers for sewage treatment? What happens when local cultural adaptation means some community excludes someone from the commons?

These are real tensions. They don't have clean answers. The framework engages them directly in [BLUEPRINT.md](BLUEPRINT.md) under "Tensions and Tradeoffs." The short version: ecological limits function like constitutional rights -- they are not subject to majority override. Necessary-but-undesirable work gets automated first, then rotated, then compensated. Cultural adaptation is permitted *within* the commons, not as a tool to deny commons access.

The tensions aren't bugs. They're the hardest design problems, and ignoring them is how systems fail.

## The Mycelial Strategy

In 2007, researcher Paul Hawken published a book arguing that the world's largest social movement already existed -- millions of organizations working on sustainability, social justice, and indigenous rights -- and nobody had noticed because it had no name, no leader, and no central address. He called it "blessed unrest." Then he built a website, WiserEarth, and catalogued 114,994 of those organizations across 243 countries.

Then the funding ran out, and the whole thing disappeared over a weekend in 2014.

The lesson is not that the idea was wrong. The lesson is about infrastructure -- specifically, two kinds of failure that have to be taken seriously together.

The technical failure: depending on a single server or a single funding source. When WiserEarth's money ran out, there was nothing to fall back on.

The social failure: the code was released as open source when the site shut down. Nobody self-hosted it. Every federated protocol -- email, XMPP, ActivityPub -- eventually concentrates around a few large nodes, because running your own server is hard and most people don't care enough to do it. The ideologically pure version of decentralization often means the realistic version fails.

The Mycelial Strategy takes both failures seriously.

The name comes from mycelia -- the underground fungal networks that connect trees, transfer nutrients, and allow a forest to function as something closer to a single organism than a collection of individuals. No central command. No single point of failure. But also not a flat chaos of unconnected nodes. A web of trust and exchange.

The approach: a distributed network of people and organizations building working alternatives to systems that don't work -- not arguing about theory, but running pilots, writing code, keeping books, growing food, running clinics. The goal before any transfer of power is to have working replacements already operating at small scale. You can't migrate to a system that doesn't exist yet.

The network is **distributed, not leaderless**. "Leaderless" is a myth -- Jo Freeman documented in 1972 that refusing to formalize leadership just creates informal leadership without accountability ("The Tyranny of Structurelessness"). The network has leaders: the people doing the work. The goal is to make that leadership visible, accountable, and replaceable -- not to pretend it doesn't exist.

The network is **transparent, not naive about transparency**. Open by default means the framework can be read by anyone, including opponents. If the idea can't survive being seen, it doesn't deserve to succeed. But transparency has failure modes: flooding, concern trolling, co-option, harassment of contributors. These are managed actively, not wished away.

The network **demonstrates rather than argues**. The primary pre-transfer activity is running proof-of-concept systems at local scale: community energy grids, cooperative businesses, participatory budgets, mutual aid networks, open-source health clinics. When conditions shift, scaling up is not a leap of faith -- it's expanding what already works.

## What Already Exists

This is not starting from zero. The network Paul Hawken mapped in 2007 still exists, mostly. It just hasn't been connected to a shared framework.

Living examples:
- Mondragon Cooperative (Basque Country): 80,000 worker-owners, 12 billion euros in annual revenue -- also Fagor's 2013 bankruptcy and some uncomfortable reliance on non-member temps. Real model, with real limits.
- Kerala (India): High human development indicators (literacy near 100%, low infant mortality, long life expectancy) on a low GDP. Shows that wellbeing and economic output are separable.
- Zapatista Autonomous Municipalities (Mexico): Indigenous communities running their own schools, clinics, courts, and governance since 1994. Imperfect, contested, but three decades old.
- Preston Model (UK): Community wealth building through anchor institutions -- local hospitals and universities prioritizing local suppliers, local hiring, local banking. Boring. Effective.
- Community Land Trusts: 300+ in the US, 250+ in England/Wales, more worldwide. Permanently affordable housing through shared ownership of land. Proven at meaningful scale in multiple countries.
- Rural Electric Cooperatives: 900+ member-owned utilities serving 42 million Americans. Cooperative energy infrastructure that has existed for 80 years.
- Decidim (Barcelona): Open-source participatory democracy platform used in 7,000+ cities worldwide for participatory budgeting and policy co-creation.

These aren't proofs that the full framework works. They're proofs that the components are not fantasy.

## The Invitation

This framework belongs to no one and to everyone.

It needs thinkers who will challenge the assumptions. Historians who know what has been tried and what failed and why. People who can design actual systems -- voting infrastructure, food distribution logistics, energy grids. Artists who can make this vision tangible enough to feel. Organizers who can connect it to existing movements. And especially skeptics who will break it -- because the failure modes you find before implementation are the ones you can actually fix.

If you're looking for a polished, finished ideology: this isn't that. If you're looking for a place to bring your specific knowledge to bear on a real and pressing problem -- the machines are taking the work, and the question of who benefits is open -- then pull requests are open.

Start at the [CRITIQUE.md](CRITIQUE.md) if you're skeptical. That's what it's there for.
