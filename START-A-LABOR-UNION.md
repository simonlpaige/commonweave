# How to start a labor union

A short, plain-language guide for people who want to organize a labor union, especially in a building trade. It is a companion to the [Labor-for-housing guide](LABOR-FOR-HOUSING-GUIDE.md), which explains why a labor union is one of the three legs of the labor-for-housing pipeline.

This guide does not replace legal counsel or an experienced organizer. Labor law is jurisdiction-specific, retaliation is real, and the rules change depending on whether you are in private-sector employment, public-sector employment, the construction trades, or organizing as independent contractors. Get help from people who have done this before. The AFL-CIO Organizing Department, individual international unions, and Jobs with Justice all do intake calls.

What this guide does is shorten the distance between "I want to organize" and "I know what the next three months look like."

---

## Three different things that all get called "starting a union"

Before any of the steps, decide which of these you are actually doing. The path is different for each.

**1. Organize a workplace under an existing union.** You and your coworkers want to join an established international union (the Carpenters, the Teamsters, IBEW, SEIU, the UAW, the Communications Workers, and so on). The international does the legal heavy lifting. You do the workplace organizing. This is the most common path and the one with the highest success rate.

**2. Charter a new local of an existing international.** No local exists in your area for the trade you work in, but the international does. You approach the international's organizing department with a list of workers, a defined jurisdiction, and a project, and they charter a new local. This is the path the [Labor-for-housing guide](LABOR-FOR-HOUSING-GUIDE.md) describes for places where the building trades have been hollowed out.

**3. Form an independent union from scratch.** No existing union covers your work, or no existing union is interested. You form your own legal entity, file your own paperwork, and bargain on your own. This is harder, but it has worked recently for tech workers, video game workers, graduate students, museum workers, and gig workers whose categories did not exist when the big unions were formed. Examples: Alphabet Workers Union, Amazon Labor Union, Independent Drivers Guild.

Most readers of this guide will be doing path 1 or path 2. Path 3 is real but takes more legal infrastructure than a short guide can provide.

## The seven steps, for path 1 (organize a workplace under an existing union)

### 1. Talk to one or two coworkers you trust, quietly

Do not start with a meeting. Start with two conversations. Find out whether the people who work alongside you have the same complaints. If they do not, you do not have a union, you have a personal frustration. That is fine, but it is not what you are organizing.

Talk away from work. Off-clock, off-property, off any device the employer pays for. Phones in another room. Texts go on subpoenable record.

### 2. Find an organizer

Most successful drives have a paid organizer from an international union behind them. The organizer's job is to keep the campaign on track, run training, advise on legal questions, and handle the parts of the National Labor Relations Board (NLRB) process that the workers themselves should not have to learn from scratch.

You find one by calling an international union you think fits your industry. The AFL-CIO has an "Organizing" intake form on their site. So does most every international. A first call is free and confidential. Tell them what you do, where, how many coworkers, and what you want to change. They will tell you whether they are interested. If one says no, call another. Different internationals have different appetites and different jurisdictions.

For building trades, the relevant internationals are: United Brotherhood of Carpenters, Laborers' International (LIUNA), International Brotherhood of Electrical Workers (IBEW), United Association of Plumbers and Pipefitters (UA), International Union of Painters and Allied Trades (IUPAT), International Association of Bridge, Structural, Ornamental and Reinforcing Iron Workers, Sheet Metal Air Rail and Transportation Workers (SMART), Operating Engineers (IUOE), Bricklayers (BAC), and the Roofers (UURWAW).

### 3. Build the organizing committee

The organizing committee is the group of workers who run the drive from inside the workplace. Three to ten people, usually. They have to be a representative cross-section: every shift, every department, every demographic, every floor, every building. A committee made entirely of one shift, one job classification, or one identity will lose because the rest of the workers will not see themselves in the campaign.

Each committee member is responsible for talking to a defined group of coworkers (usually ten to twenty). They report what they hear back to the committee. The committee meets regularly, usually weekly, off-site.

### 4. Map the workplace

Make a list of every worker the union would represent. Name, job title, shift, department, contact info, where they stand on the union (yes / lean yes / undecided / lean no / no), and who knows them best. This list is the heart of the campaign. It is the difference between organizing and hoping.

A good organizer will help build this. A great committee member will know every worker's first name, kid's name, and what shift they hate.

### 5. Get authorization cards from a strong majority

In the US, an authorization card is a signed statement that the worker wants the union to represent them. To trigger an NLRB election, you need 30 percent of the workers in the proposed bargaining unit to sign cards. To win an election, you need a majority on election day, which usually means going in with at least 65 to 70 percent signed up, because some of those signers will flip during the employer's anti-union campaign.

Cards are signed in private conversations between organizing committee members and their coworkers, almost never at meetings. They are stored somewhere not in the workplace and not on workplace systems.

In some sectors, the employer agrees to recognize the union once a majority of cards are signed, with no election. This is "card check" recognition and it requires either a friendly employer or a state law that allows it (a few do, for a few sectors). Most US private-sector campaigns go to election.

### 6. File for an election (or get voluntary recognition)

Once the cards are in, the organizer files a petition with the NLRB. The election happens, in most cases, three to six weeks later. During those weeks the employer will run an anti-union campaign. This is the part of the process that takes the most stamina from the committee. There are entire books on what to expect; read at least one before the petition is filed. *Labor's Story in the United States* (Dubofsky and McCartin) gives the historical sweep. *Secrets of a Successful Organizer* (Bradbury, Brenner, Slaughter) is the practical training manual; most NLRB-side organizers have read it.

Election day, you need a majority of votes cast (not a majority of the unit). Workers who do not show up to vote do not count.

### 7. Bargain a first contract

Winning the election is not winning the union. Winning the first contract is. Statistically, almost half of newly-certified unions in the US never get a first contract, because the employer drags negotiations out for years. The fix is to plan for the contract campaign before the election, with a credible threat of action (informational picketing, slowdowns, public pressure, strike) backing the bargaining table.

This is where the international union earns its dues, and where workers learn that organizing is not a single event but a practice you do indefinitely.

## Path 2: charter a new local of an existing international

This is the path most relevant to the labor-for-housing pipeline. The structure is similar to path 1 but the conversation with the international is different.

You walk in with three things:
- **A defined geography and trade.** "We want to charter a new Carpenters local for [city or county], covering residential and light-commercial work."
- **A list of interested workers.** Not signed cards, just names and trade backgrounds. The international wants to know whether the warm bodies exist.
- **A defined project or pipeline.** "We have a CLT partner who has committed land for ten homes over three years. The new local would be the labor partner on those builds, plus organize private-market residential work in the same trade." A specific project is the difference between "we want a local" and "we have a local."

The international's organizing department will tell you whether they are interested in chartering. If they are, they will assign a regional staffer to help you through the process. The charter itself is administrative, but the relationships and bylaws take six to twelve months to set up.

Once chartered, the new local immediately has access to: the international's training infrastructure (a Joint Apprenticeship Training Committee, or JATC, in most trades), the international's master agreement and bargaining patterns, the international's strike fund and pension fund, and the international's legal department.

This is most of why path 2 is preferred over path 3 for trades work in any country with mature industrial unions. The training infrastructure and pension fund alone are worth decades of independent organizing.

## Path 3: form an independent union

If no existing international fits, or none is interested, you can form an independent union. The legal structure in the US is usually a "labor organization" under the NLRA, which is its own corporate filing distinct from a 501(c)(3) or an LLC. You will need:
- Bylaws (a labor-union bylaws template is harder to find than a nonprofit one; *Democracy Is Power* by Mike Parker is a starting reference).
- A constitution that defines membership, officers, and dues.
- An annual LM-2 or LM-3 financial filing with the US Department of Labor's Office of Labor-Management Standards.
- A bonding requirement for officers handling union funds.
- A treasurer who can read a balance sheet.

Independent unions can affiliate later with a federation (the AFL-CIO's structure, the Strategic Organizing Center's structure, the IWW for some sectors). They can also stay independent. There is no rule that says you have to affiliate.

Recent successful independent unions: Amazon Labor Union (Staten Island, 2022), Independent Drivers Guild (NYC rideshare drivers), Alphabet Workers Union (Google, 2021, affiliated with the Communication Workers of America after founding). All three started independent and went different directions on affiliation.

## What this costs and how long it takes

For path 1 (organizing under an existing international), the campaign costs the workers nothing in money. It costs about **6 to 18 months** of organizing-committee time, off-clock, with weekly committee meetings and constant one-on-one conversations. The international pays the staff organizer.

For path 2 (chartering a new local), add about **6 to 12 months** of administrative setup once the international has agreed. The international fronts the chartering costs. The new local pays them back over time out of dues revenue once members are signed up.

For path 3 (independent union), figure **$5,000 to $25,000** in legal and filing costs to set up, and **$25,000 to $100,000 a year** in operating costs once it has members. Independent unions usually fund themselves through dues (1 to 2 percent of member wages is typical) plus startup grants from labor-friendly foundations.

## Where this gets stuck

Three places, again roughly by frequency.

**Retaliation.** It is illegal in the US to fire someone for union activity, but it happens constantly, and the legal remedies are weak (back pay, eventual reinstatement). The fix is not to count on the law to protect you. The fix is to organize fast enough and broadly enough that retaliation against any one worker triggers a credible response from the rest. Committees that organize quietly for too long are easier to pick off.

**Employer anti-union campaigns work.** Most US workers go through one or more "captive audience" meetings during a drive, where the employer makes the case against the union. In about a third of campaigns, this swings enough votes to lose the election. The fix is not to wish the meetings away (the law allows them, for now). The fix is to inoculate workers in advance: tell them exactly what the employer is going to say and why, weeks before the employer says it. Inoculation works.

**The first contract never comes.** As above, this is the single biggest reason new unions fail to deliver value to their members. The fix is to plan the contract campaign before the election. A union that wins the vote and then tries to figure out the contract strategy is starting six months late.

## What to read next, and who to talk to

**Read.**
- *Secrets of a Successful Organizer* (Bradbury, Brenner, Slaughter, Labor Notes). The practical bible for new organizers.
- *No Shortcuts: Organizing for Power in the New Gilded Age* (Jane McAlevey). The strategic argument for "deep organizing" instead of mobilization.
- *Reviving the Strike* (Joe Burns). On why the strike is still the only real source of worker power.
- For building trades specifically: the AFL-CIO Building and Construction Trades Department publishes a primer on Project Labor Agreements that every CLT should read before talking to a local.

**Talk to.**
- An international union whose jurisdiction covers your trade. Their organizing departments do free intake calls. The list of building-trades internationals is in step 2 above.
- The Workers Defense Project (in Texas), the Carpenters' Regional Council in your region, or the AFL-CIO state federation. State feds often know which locals are healthy and which are dormant.
- An existing union local in a related trade. They usually know the lay of the land for trades that do not yet have a local.
- The Commonweave directory, which lists about 1,500 unions globally as of this writing, including the major US internationals and many international federations. Find them in the [directory](https://commonweave.earth/directory.html) under the labor framework area.

The Commonweave project is happy to help with the directory side of this. If you organize a new local or an independent union and want to be listed, [email us](mailto:hello@commonweave.earth). We do not give legal advice and we do not run campaigns. We keep the map up to date so the people doing the actual work can find each other.
