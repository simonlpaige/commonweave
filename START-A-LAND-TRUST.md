# How to start a community land trust

A short, plain-language guide for people who want to start a CLT in their own town. It is a companion to the [Labor-for-housing guide](LABOR-FOR-HOUSING-GUIDE.md), which explains what a CLT is and why it is one of the three legs of the labor-for-housing pipeline.

This guide does not replace legal counsel. Starting a CLT means filing for nonprofit status in your state, applying for federal tax exemption, and writing bylaws and ground leases that will outlive you. Get an attorney who has done at least one of these before. The Grounded Solutions Network keeps a list. Your state's nonprofit association keeps another.

What this guide does is shorten the distance between "I want to start one" and "I know what the next three months look like."

---

## What you are actually starting

A community land trust is a nonprofit corporation that owns land and leases it to residents under a long ground lease (usually 99 years, renewable). The resident owns the building, the CLT owns the dirt. When the resident moves out, the home resells under a formula the CLT controls, which keeps it affordable for the next family forever.

The point of forming the entity is not the entity. The point is the **land you put into it**. A CLT with no land is paperwork. A CLT with two donated lots and a plan is a beginning.

So while you are doing the legal work below, you should also be looking for land. The two tracks run in parallel.

## The seven steps, in rough order

### 1. Find five to nine other people

A CLT is governed by a "tripartite" board: one-third residents of CLT homes, one-third community members from the surrounding neighborhood, one-third public-interest representatives (a local elected official, a housing nonprofit director, an attorney, a lender). At the very start you will not have residents yet, because you have no homes, so the first board is two-thirds community members and one-third public-interest. The resident seats fill in once the first homes are occupied.

You need at least five people willing to be on the founding board. Nine is better. Fewer than five and one person leaving kills the org. More than fifteen and meetings stop working.

The community-member seats should come from the actual neighborhood the CLT will serve, not from across town. This is the whole "community" in community land trust. Boards that skip this step end up with CLTs that do things to a neighborhood instead of for one.

### 2. Pick the geography

Decide what neighborhood, what city, or what region the CLT covers. This goes in the bylaws and is hard to change later. Most CLTs serve one neighborhood (a few square miles) or one city. Some, especially rural ones, cover a county or a few counties.

Smaller geography is easier to govern and harder to scale. Larger geography is the reverse. The Dudley Street Neighborhood Initiative in Boston picked a neighborhood. Champlain Housing Trust in Vermont picked a region. Both work. Pick what matches your actual relationships.

### 3. Incorporate as a state nonprofit

In the US, you incorporate as a nonprofit corporation in your state. The forms are short. Filing fees are usually $50 to $200. Your state's Secretary of State website has the form. Use a name that says what you are: "Westside Community Land Trust" beats "Westside Housing Initiative" because the former tells a stranger what you do.

You will need a registered agent (a person or service that accepts legal mail at a real address). Many attorneys serve as registered agent for free for nonprofits they help form.

### 4. Write bylaws

Bylaws are the operating rules of the corporation: how the board is elected, how long terms are, who can vote on what, what counts as a quorum, how the resident-third gets seated once you have residents, what voting threshold changes the bylaws themselves.

Do not write these from scratch. The Grounded Solutions Network publishes a model CLT bylaws template. Most state nonprofit associations also have one. Start with a template, change the parts that matter for your situation, and have an attorney review the result. Writing original bylaws is how you accidentally lock yourself out of your own corporation in year three.

### 5. Apply for federal 501(c)(3) tax exemption

This is Form 1023 or, if your projected revenue is under $50,000 a year for the first three years, Form 1023-EZ. The EZ version is cheap and fast (about $275 and a few weeks). The full 1023 is more expensive (about $600) and takes three to six months. Most starting CLTs file the EZ.

You will need to have your bylaws done, your board named, and your state incorporation complete before you file 1023. The IRS asks for all three in the application.

While you wait for the determination letter, you cannot legally claim donations are tax-deductible. Most CLTs hold off on serious fundraising until the letter arrives, or they fundraise through a fiscal sponsor (a larger nonprofit that holds your donations for you in exchange for a small fee, usually 7 to 10 percent).

### 6. Get land

This is the hard part. The CLT exists to hold land. You need some.

Common paths in:
- **A donation.** A landowner with vacant lots, a church with surplus land, an estate looking for a charitable deduction, a city with tax-foreclosed property. A 501(c)(3) status letter unlocks the tax-deduction path for the donor and is usually the trigger for these conversations.
- **A municipal partnership.** Many cities have land banks that hold tax-foreclosed or otherwise abandoned property and will transfer to a CLT at low or zero cost in exchange for affordability commitments. This is how Dudley Neighbors Inc started.
- **A purchase.** With grant funding (HOME funds, CDBG funds, state housing trust fund money) or a loan from a community development financial institution (CDFI), the CLT buys land outright. This is slower because grant cycles are slow.
- **A long-term lease from a public owner.** A school district, a transit agency, or a city leases land to the CLT for 99 years at $1 a year. The CLT then builds on top under a sub-lease to residents. This avoids the upfront purchase entirely and is becoming more common.

The CLT does not have to start with much. Two lots is a real CLT. Five is a movement.

### 7. Build, partner, or rehab the first home

Once you have land, you need housing on it. Three paths:

- **Build new** with a partner. Habitat for Humanity has done this with CLTs in many cities. So have local nonprofit developers, faith-based builders, and union-led building programs (this is the entire point of the [Labor-for-housing guide](LABOR-FOR-HOUSING-GUIDE.md)).
- **Rehab existing.** If your land donation came with an old building on it, rehab is often cheaper than new construction. Some of the most successful early CLTs (Cooper Square in Manhattan, Dudley Neighbors in Boston) started with rehab.
- **Move existing housing onto the land.** A few CLTs have accepted donations of houses being moved off other parcels. This is unusual but real.

Whichever path, the first home is the hardest. The second is half as hard. By the fifth, the CLT has a process.

## What this costs and how long it takes

A bare-bones startup: about **$1,500 to $5,000** in filing fees, registered agent costs, and legal review, plus **6 to 12 months** of part-time volunteer work from the founding board. Add another $5,000 to $20,000 if you pay an attorney for substantial help drafting bylaws and the ground lease (worth it).

A startup that includes hiring a part-time executive director and paying for a real legal package, before any land is acquired: **$50,000 to $100,000**, raised before the CLT can do anything visible. This is realistic for a CLT that intends to scale past five homes.

The land itself is a separate budget. A two-lot pilot in a low-cost market might cost the CLT zero, if the land is donated. The same pilot in a hot market might require $200,000 to $400,000 in acquisition costs.

## Where this gets stuck

Three places, listed roughly by frequency.

**Land never arrives.** The CLT is incorporated, has 501(c)(3) status, has bylaws, has a board, and has no land. Three years pass and there is still no land. This is the most common way CLTs die quietly. The fix is to start the land conversations on day one, not after the legal entity is built. The legal entity is a vehicle for holding land. If you have no land in sight by month twelve, something is wrong with the relationships, not with the paperwork.

**The board fractures.** A nine-person board with strong opinions about housing has a not-small chance of splitting in year two over a specific decision (usually the resale formula, occasionally a tenant dispute). Bylaws that spell out how disagreements get resolved, term limits that rotate the board over time, and a real process for adding new members all reduce the chance the fracture is fatal. Skipping any of these is how a CLT dies in year three.

**Funding stalls before the first home.** Grants take time. Even friendly grants take time. A CLT that has not raised any operating capital before applying for development funding will run out of volunteer energy before the first home is occupied. Raise some money before you need it.

## What to read next, and who to talk to

**Read.**
- The Grounded Solutions Network's *CLT Technical Manual* (it is several hundred pages, most of which are templates you can copy).
- *The Community Land Trust Reader* (Davis, ed.), if you want the history and the philosophy.
- *On Common Ground: International Perspectives on the Community Land Trust* (Davis, Algoed, Hernandez-Torrales, eds., 2020), if you want the international comparisons.

**Talk to.**
- Your nearest existing CLT. Most will give you an hour. Find them in the [Commonweave directory](https://commonweave.org/directory.html) by filtering for CLTs in your state or country. The directory has about 350 land trusts as of this writing, growing.
- The Grounded Solutions Network. They run an annual conference, a member network, and will sometimes do introductions.
- A local affordable-housing nonprofit. They probably already have the relationships you need (with the city, with funders, with builders) and may be willing to fiscally sponsor you while you get on your feet.

The Commonweave project is happy to help with the directory side of this. We are not lawyers, we are not funders, and we will not pretend to be either. If you start a CLT and want to be in the directory, [email us](mailto:hello@commonweave.org) and we will add you. If you find errors in our entries, tell us and we will fix them.
